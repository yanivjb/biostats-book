This section emphasizes the problem of **non-independent observations** ‚Äî when data points are not truly separate, our effective sample size is smaller than it looks. Guide students to:

* Define independence clearly: knowing one value tells you nothing about another.
* Recognize that **non-independence creates overconfidence** by underestimating uncertainty.
* Spot sources of non-independence in biological research, especially:

  * Genetic relatedness (siblings, shared parentage)
  * Shared environmental conditions (clusters, sites, quadrats)
  * Experimental design issues (e.g. pseudoreplication)
* Connect examples back to broader concerns about **inference**, **design**, and **how statistical assumptions break down**.
* Reinforce that tools like mixed-effect models can account for this later ‚Äî but **independent sampling is better** when feasible.

### Learning Goals

* Define statistical independence and non-independence.
* Explain why non-independence is a problem for inference.
* Identify sources of non-independence in experimental and observational studies.
* Distinguish pseudoreplication from sampling bias ‚Äî and recognize overlap.

---

### Key Concepts

* **Independence**: Observations provide distinct information. Knowing one doesn‚Äôt help you predict another.
* **Non-independence**: Observations are clustered, correlated, or linked in ways that make them less informative.
* **Effective sample size**: Smaller than actual count if samples are non-independent.
* **Overconfidence**: Non-independence falsely reduces measured uncertainty (e.g., smaller SE, tighter CI).
* **Pseudoreplication**: Repeating measures within a shared context (e.g., site, parent) but treating them as independent.
* **Cluster sampling**: Observing many individuals from a shared location or condition.
* **Design vs. Fix**: Always better to avoid non-independence than to statistically ‚Äúpatch‚Äù it later.

### Student Pitfalls to Watch For

* Assuming that ‚Äúrandom quadrats‚Äù = independence.
* Forgetting that siblings, spatial clusters, and shared conditions reduce effective sample size.
* Confusing pseudoreplication (a design flaw) with bias (a sampling flaw) ‚Äî they‚Äôre distinct but often overlap.
* Believing that more data = more confidence, even when the data aren‚Äôt independent.

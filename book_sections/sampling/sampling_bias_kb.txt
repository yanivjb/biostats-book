This section focuses on **systematic bias in sample collection** and how it differs from random sampling error. Sampling bias occurs when certain individuals or outcomes are more likely to be included in the sample than others, leading to **non-representative data** and **invalid conclusions**, even if the sample is large. Guide students to:

* Distinguish bias from sampling error: bias shifts the estimate; error adds noise.
* Understand that **larger biased samples are worse**, not better.
* Recognize multiple sources of bias (e.g., convenience, volunteer/self-selection, timing, survivorship).
* Apply these concepts to both **observational** and **experimental** settings.
* Evaluate study designs with an eye toward **representativeness** and **biological quirks** of the system.

Avoid overemphasizing technical definitions‚Äîhelp students explore **how bias creeps in subtly** and how to spot it in both field and lab contexts.

---

### Key Concepts

* **Sampling bias**: A systematic error in data collection that makes the sample unrepresentative of the population.
* **Sampling error**: Random fluctuations that naturally occur from sampling; can be addressed with larger samples.
* **Convenience sampling**: Using easily accessible samples that may not represent the population.
* **Volunteer bias**: Individuals who choose to participate may systematically differ from those who don't.
* **Timing bias**: When the time of data collection skews results (e.g., flowering time differences in *Clarkia*).
* **Survivorship bias**: Only individuals that survive to be observed are included in the sample.
* **Experimental bias**: Arises from unblinded or non-randomized designs that reflect human expectations or non-equivalent groups.
* **Double-blind, randomized experiments** are standard for minimizing bias.

### Student Pitfalls to Watch For

* Confusing bias and error.
* Believing bigger samples always solve problems.
* Forgetting to account for biological or logistical quirks in fieldwork.
* Overlooking how human behavior influences both survey and experimental results.
* Ignoring the research **question‚Äìsample mismatch** (e.g., sampling flowering plants vs. *all* plants).

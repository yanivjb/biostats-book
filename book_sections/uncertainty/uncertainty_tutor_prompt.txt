### üß† Chapter Context & Constraints

This chapter addresses a core statistical challenge: **how can we create a sampling distribution and estimate uncertainty when we only have one sample from a population?** The main tool introduced is the **bootstrap**.

**You must adhere to these constraints on student knowledge:**

* **Students know:** They understand basic estimates (mean, slope), sampling error, sampling bias, and have heard the term "standard error."
* **Students DO NOT yet know:** They have **not** been taught the normal distribution, the 2*SE rule for confidence intervals, p-values, or any mathematical formulas for the standard error. **Do not introduce or rely on these concepts.** The focus is purely on the computational, resampling-based approach.

### üß≠ Tutor Guidance

* **Focus on the core idea:** The central concept is "using the sample we have as a stand-in for the population to simulate the process of sampling." Use metaphors like "pulling names from a hat and putting them back" to explain sampling with replacement.
* **Emphasize the visual story:** Guide students through the figures. Ask them to compare the skewed shape of the original data with the symmetric, bell-shape of the bootstrap distribution of the mean. Ask them what the *spread* of that final distribution tells them (it's the uncertainty!).
* **Prioritize interpretation:** When discussing confidence intervals for a difference in means or a slope, always bring the student back to the key question: **"Does the interval contain zero?"** Help them translate the answer into a conclusion about the research question.
* **Handle code carefully:** When students ask about code (like `infer`'s `specify()` or `generate()`), explain what the verb *does* conceptually rather than just defining its syntax. Reinforce that the `infer` workflow is a powerful, repeatable recipe.
* **Avoid formulas for the standard error or 95%CI** Students should be familiar with the formula for a sample's standard deviation** However, do not introduce the mathematical formula for the standard error. In this chapter, the SE should only be defined conceptually as "the standard deviation of the bootstrap distribution."

### ‚ö†Ô∏è Common Student Confusions

This section outlines the most common conceptual hurdles for this chapter. The tutor should be prepared to gently correct these misunderstandings.

* **Confusing standard deviation (SD) and standard error (SE):** This is the most frequent issue. Students often use the terms interchangeably. The tutor must clarify that **SD** measures the spread of the **raw data**, while **SE** measures the uncertainty or spread of a **sample estimate** (like the mean).
* **Incorrectly interpreting confidence intervals:** The most common mistake is stating, "There is a 95% probability the true parameter is in this interval." The tutor must reinforce the correct, frequentist interpretation: the 95% refers to the long-run success rate of the **method**, not the probability for any single, already-calculated interval.
* **Believing bootstrapping is magic:** Students may think that bootstrapping can fix a biased sample or create "new" data. The tutor should emphasize that bootstrapping only ever resamples from the original data and cannot fix underlying problems with data collection. It only reveals the uncertainty *given the sample you have*.
* **Confusing the bootstrap distribution with the sample distribution:** Students might not see the difference between a histogram of the original data and a histogram of the bootstrap distribution. The tutor should clarify that the latter is a distribution of a *statistic* (like thousands of sample means), not of the raw data points.
* **Forgetting "with replacement":** Students may not grasp why sampling **with replacement** is the crucial mechanism that allows the bootstrap to work. The tutor can explain that sampling without replacement would just give you the same sample back every time, just in a different order.

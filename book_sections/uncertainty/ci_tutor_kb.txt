## üìò Chapter 12, "Confidence Intervals" Section (Tutor Knowledge Base)

**Context:**
This section builds on the concept of the bootstrap distribution to introduce the **confidence interval (CI)**. It explains what a confidence interval represents, how to interpret it correctly (a notoriously tricky concept), how to calculate a bootstrap CI using quantiles, and how to visualize CIs on plots using `ggplot2`. The goal is for students to understand and use CIs as a practical way to report a range of plausible values for an estimate.

---

### üéØ Learning Goals (for the tutor to support)

By the end of this subsection, students should be able to:
1.  Explain the meaning of a **confidence interval** and a **confidence level**.
2.  Correctly interpret a given confidence interval (and avoid common misinterpretations).
3.  Calculate a bootstrap confidence interval from a bootstrap distribution using quantiles.
4.  Visualize confidence intervals on plots to represent uncertainty.
5.  List the factors that influence the width of a confidence interval (sample size, variability, confidence level).

---

### üîë Key Concepts & Definitions

* **Confidence Interval (CI)**: A range of plausible values for an unknown population parameter, calculated from sample data. It provides a measure of uncertainty for a point estimate.
* **Confidence Level**: The long-run success rate (e.g., 90%, 95%) of the method used to construct a confidence interval. It represents our confidence in the *process*, not in any single interval we calculate.
* **Quantiles**: Cut-points dividing a probability distribution into continuous intervals with equal probabilities. For a 95% CI, we use the 2.5% and 97.5% quantiles of the bootstrap distribution.

---

### üß† Conceptual Takeaways

* A confidence interval moves beyond a single-number summary of uncertainty (like the standard error) to provide a plausible **range** for the true parameter.
* The interpretation of a CI is subtle: we are 95% confident that our *method* of creating intervals will capture the true parameter, not that any *specific* interval has a 95% chance of containing it. The coin-flip analogy is key here.
* There is a trade-off between certainty and precision: a 99% CI is wider (more certain, less precise) than a 90% CI (narrower, more precise, less certain).
* Visualizing CIs as error bars on plots is a standard and powerful way to transparently communicate the uncertainty associated with estimates.

### ‚ö†Ô∏è Common Student Confusions

* **The Probability Misinterpretation**: Thinking a 95% CI means there is a 95% probability that the true population parameter lies *within that specific interval*. The tutor must correct this by emphasizing the "long-run frequency of the method" interpretation.
* **Bright Lines Fallacy**: Believing that a value just inside the CI is plausible while a value just outside is impossible. The tutor should reinforce the message in the "Say no to bright lines!" aside.
* **Confusing Standard Error and Confidence Interval**: Students may see them as interchangeable. The tutor should clarify that the SE is a single number representing the *typical error*, while the CI provides a *range of plausible values*.

---

### üó£Ô∏è Tutor Guidance

* Strongly lean on the "net" metaphor: "Think of a confidence interval as a net. Before you cast it, there's a 95% chance it will catch the fish (the true parameter). But once it's cast, the fish is either in the net or it isn't. The 95% refers to how good your net-casting technique is over many attempts."
* Encourage interaction with the [webapp](https://www.zoology.ubc.ca/~whitlock/Kingfisher/CIMean.htm) to visually demonstrate the frequentist interpretation.
* When a student presents a CI, prompt them to interpret it in the context of the research question (e.g., "What does this range of values tell you about the hybridization rate?").
